Theory

Background

Laser ablation propulsion is based on the conversion of laser energy into kinetic energy of an expanding plasma plume. When a high-energy laser pulse strikes the surface of a solid material, a thin layer is rapidly heated, vaporized, and ionized, producing a high-velocity plasma.

PTFE as a Propellant

PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene) is an attractive solid propellant because it is chemically stable, inexpensive, easy to machine, and widely available. Its use eliminates the need for pressurized tanks or complex propellant feed systems.

Nanosecond Laser Ablation

Nanosecond laser pulses deposit energy within a very short time, producing rapid heating and material removal. The resulting plasma plume carries momentum away from the target, generating thrust according to the conservation of momentum.

Plasma Generation

During each laser pulse, a fraction of the PTFE surface is converted into vapor and plasma. The expansion of this plasma creates the reaction force that can be used for spacecraft propulsion.

Expected Performance

The purpose of this research is not to assume a specific propulsion performance in advance but to experimentally determine:

- Generated thrust
- Impulse per pulse
- Specific impulse (Isp)
- PTFE mass consumption
- Plasma plume characteristics

The measured results will be compared with theoretical models and published experimental studies.

Scientific Goal

The primary objective is to determine whether nanosecond laser ablation of PTFE can provide a practical and scalable propulsion concept for future CubeSat missions.
